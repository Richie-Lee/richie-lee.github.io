Econometric Panel Data Modelling: Albert Heijn (Ahold Delhaize)

Addressing unobserved heterogeneity with micro-econometrics in collaboration with Albert Heijn (Ahold Delhaize)

Posted by Richie (contributor) on Sunday, May 1, 2022

TL:DR

In collaboration with Albert Heijn, this study explores the dynamics between product labels and consumer buying behaviour. The study successfully addressed the challenges posed by panel data, such as unobserved heterogeneity, omitted variable bias, and seasonality, demonstrating the usefulness of the micro-econometric toolkit.

The positive reception of the findings and methodologies led to an invitation to present the results at Albert Heijn’s Data Science department’s Knowledge Exchange initiative. The main contribution of the study is the proof-of-concept and simplifiction of micro-econometric methods for these specific use-cases.

Context

In the competitive landscape of the supermarket industry, understanding consumer behaviour is crucial for Dutch market leader Albert Heijn (parent: Ahold Delhaize) in developing effective strategies to improve revenue and profitability. This project specifically focuses on measuring the impacts of product labels on consumer buying behaviour.

Data

The analysis in this post is based on a panel dataset that spans a time horizon of approximately 2 years, with monthly observations (approximately 24 data points). The dataset consists of information on 118 unique products, including units sold (proportion of total sales), prices, product categories (Dairy, Fruits, Meat, Soda), product attributes (e.g. Vegetarian, Vegan, HighFiber, Plantbased, LowSugar) and corresponding weights/units. Moreover, to account for seasonal variations, we include seasonal dummy variables in our analysis.

Methodology

Micro-econometric models

To account for unobserved heterogeneity and omitted variable biases, we employ methods from the field of Micro-econometrics:

OLS (Baseline)

  • Advantages: Simple, Parsimonious
  • Disadvantages: Doesn’t capture heterogeneity

Fixed Effect (FE) models control for unobserved individual-specific characteristics by including individual-specific dummy variables, enabling estimation from within-individual variation in the data. The method used is the Least squares dummy variable (LSDV) regression, providing consistent estimators if the data-generating process aligns with both the FE and RE model.

  • Advantages: Captures unobserved heterogeneity and enables within individual estimation of time-varying effects.
  • Disadvantages: No parameter estimation for non-time-varying variables

Random Effect (RE) models treat unobserved individual-specific characteristics as random variables, assuming their independence from the observed predictors, and estimate coefficients by accounting for both within-individual and between-individual variations in the data using the efficient Generalized Least Squares (GLS) estimator.

  • Advantages: Parameter estimation for non-time-varying variables, captures individual variance
  • Disadvantages: No individual effect estimation, biased under violation of orthogonality assumption on error terms and individual effects

Group Fixed Effects (Latent Class) is an extension of fixed effect models, accounting for unobserved group-specific characteristics with dummy variables. It allows for arbitrary correlations between covariates and group-specific unobservables, and permits time-varying group effects, providing flexibility. Unlike finite mixture models, time-varying effects are not mandatory, offering the option to use time-invariant group-specific effects if needed. The clustering as well as the selection of number of clusters follows Bonhomme and Manresa (2015).

  • Advantages: Segmentation, Captures Grouped Effects, Seasonality, Detects outliers
  • Disadvantage: Normality Assumption, May not converge, Computationally heavy

Additionally, we utilise the Breusch-Pagan test and Breusch-Godfrey test to evaluate heteroskedasticity and serial correlation respectively.

Model selection

The general procedure is as follows; firstly, the Chow test is implemented to determine the poolability of the panel data. Following this, the Hausman test is implemented to decide between the FE or RE specification if poolability holds. Lastly, both the fixed and random effect models are compared against ordinary least square regression with the F test and Breusch-Pagan LM test respectively.

  • Chow Test: The Chow Test assesses whether the effects of covariates are consistent across individuals or over time. The null hypothesis H0: βik = βk assumes coefficients to be equal across all individuals for each of the k variables. It compares the sum of squared residuals from the pooled OLS and pooled OLS per individual to make the determination.

  • Durbin–Wu–Hausman test: This test (Hausman (1978)) determines which model has a better specification. If the data-generating process follows a RE model, both fixed and random effect estimates are consistent and nearly identical. However, if the data-generating process is a FE model, the RE estimate becomes inconsistent and significantly deviates from the consistent FE estimator as per Greene (2003).

  • Breusch-Pagan Lagrange Multiplier (LM) Test: (Breusch and Pagan (1980)), this test identifies whether individual-specific variance components are present. The null hypothesis H0: σ2_a = 0 is rejected if the idiosyncratic error significantly deviates from zero, indicating the presence of a significant random effect in the panel data.

  • F Test: The F test for FE determines the prevalence of individual-specific intercepts. It compares the goodness-of-fit of the FE model against the pooled least squares model to assess the appropriateness of including individual-specific intercepts in the analysis.

Results

The analysis aimed to investigate the effect of product labels on sales in a supermarket across different categories. Some examples of insights from this specific regression output we extracted from the data revealed the following:

  • Private label products had a positive effect on sales for Meat and Dairy goods but had a negative effect on sales for Soda and Fruit categories.
  • Seasonal effects were only observed in the Soda category, with higher sales during the first quarter compared to subsequent months.
  • Vegetarian labelling was found to be important for Fruit and Meat goods, consistent with the group fixed effects analysis.
  • Low fat labelling positively impacted sales for Soda and Dairy products.
  • Latent class analysis suggested broadening the range of meat brands and introducing more private branded products in the dairy category.

Model selection result: Random Effects (RE)

The Hausman Test pointed us towards the RE model, revealing its superiority over the FE model. Additionally, the F-test unveiled a significant fixed effect, suggesting its crucial impact on the data. The Breusch-Pagan LM Test affirmed the preference for RE over a simple OLS regression. Notably, we identified heteroskedasticity and serial correlation through the Breusch-Pagan and Breusch-Godfrey Tests. To handle these complexities, we employed a robust covariance matrix, ensuring accurate and reliable results. With these adjustments, we gained valuable insights and a comprehensive understanding of the data.

Concluding remarks

In summary, this project provided valuable insights into the influence of product attributes on sales within the supermarket industry. The results underscore the significance of considering the specific product categories and the need for effective segmentation strategies. The micro-econometric methods proved suitable for this study, as they accommodated the computational demands, panel data characteristics, and factors such as individual-specific effects, seasonality, and unobserved heterogeneity.

When dealing with small sample, panel data and unobserved heterogeneity, these methods will hold promise for broader applications. If you are interested in applying similar techniques to your own work, particularly when faced with potential unobserved heterogeneity or panel data, I recommend referring to the guidelines outlined in the model selection section for a streamlined approach.
